The desk lamp in Mike's office was the only light left on in the third-floor suite at 2:00 AM. For nine years, his boutique agency had bought Facebook ads for regional retail brands, pulling in a comfortable seven figures. But over the last eighteen months, the numbers stopped making sense. Mike noticed a pattern in the Meta dashboard that his account managers were completely missing: client ad spend was climbing by 40%, but actual checkout conversions on Shopify were dipping. The platform was grading its own homework, claiming "estimated ad recall lift" and "optimized impression share" while his clients' warehouse shelves stayed entirely full.

Mike pulled up a spreadsheet for a leather goods brand he’d represented since 2018. In the early days, every dollar spent returned five dollars in revenue. Now, the dashboard claimed a healthy triple-return, but the client’s bank account was dry. The algorithm had started targeting the easiest targets—people who had already added items to their cart or visited the site three times that week—and claiming 100% of the credit for the sale. It wasn't driving new growth; it was tax-collecting on existing customers. Mike realized the entire performance marketing playbook had become an expensive illusion designed to feed the platform's stock price, not the businesses paying for it. He walked out to his car that night knowing the industry was running a massive, legalized racket.

I sat in my own office a few months later, staring at a spreadsheet that looked exactly like Mike's. For ten years, I had built and run my own agency. On paper, I was a successful founder with a decade of momentum, a team that relied on me, and a roster of clients who paid their retainers on the first of the month.

In reality, I was completely exhausted. The business was becoming less profitable every quarter because the cost to acquire a customer through paid channels had skyrocketed. More than that, I hated what we were selling. I spent my days pitching optimizations for systems I no longer believed in, forcing creative ideas into rigid, vertical ad formats that users swiped past in half a second. The passion that carried me through the first seven years had completely evaporated, replaced by a quiet dread every time my phone buzzed. I woke up every morning knowing I was turning the crank on a machine that was running out of gas.

People stay in dying businesses for the same reason they stay in bad relationships: the sunk cost fallacy. Your brain tells you that the ten years you already spent matters more than the next thirty years of your life. You look at the logo, the LLC filing, and the years of sweat equity, and you treat them like a monument that can't be touched. You convince yourself that if you just tweak the offer, change the pricing, or hire one more account executive, the old spark will come back.

It won't. You are hiding behind a broken business model because starting over forces you to look at your own reflection and admit you got comfortable. You would rather manage a slow, predictable decline than face the raw terror of a blank page. You tell yourself you are being responsible, but you are just being afraid.

True leverage belongs to the people willing to destroy their own creations when they no longer work. I closed the doors on my ten-year agency because I refused to spend the next decade defending a past that was already dead.

I started 701am, an Earned Media agency built for how people actually pay attention today. People do not buy from brands because an algorithm harassed them into submission with sixteen retargeting ads. They buy because a story caught them off guard, because a peer recommended it, or because the brand earned its way into the cultural conversation. The old playbook is broken, and everyone knows it. You can keep funding an algorithm that hates you, or you can build something people actually want to talk about. Choose one.
